title: "What Should an Agent Remember, Reveal, and Redact? The New Data Sinks and Leaks of Agentic AI"
bio: "Niloofar Mireshghallah is an assistant professor at Carnegie Mellon University, jointly appointed in the Language Technologies Institute and the Department of Engineering and Public Policy, and a core member of CyLab. Her research lies at the intersection of privacy, natural language processing, and the societal implications of machine learning, with a focus on contextual integrity and information-flow norms in LLMs and agentic systems. Previously, she was a research scientist at Meta's FAIR lab and a postdoctoral scholar at the University of Washington's Paul G. Allen School. She received her Ph.D. from UC San Diego in 2023. Her work has been covered by The Washington Post and WIRED, and she is a recipient of the NCWIT Collegiate Award and the Rising Star in Adversarial ML Award."
abstract: "GenAI is no longer a chat box. It is agents moving our data through tools, APIs, memories, and modalities. This shift creates new data sinks where sensitive information quietly accumulates, and new leak paths where it slips across contexts: persistent memories surfacing in the wrong conversation, users oversharing because they don't know what a model actually needs. Through CIMemories [ICLR 2026], we introduce a compositional benchmark for whether LLMs respect contextual integrity when drawing on persistent memory, and find that frontier models leak sensitive attributes at high rates, that violations compound with use, and that privacy-conscious prompting does not fix the underlying instability. Beyond these inference-time risks, the second half of the talk turns to memorization. We study it through a transitive lens: not whether a model reproduces a single sequence it saw, but how co-occurrences and similar-appearing data chain together during training. In Privacy Ripple Effects [ACL 2025], we characterize assisted memorization, where PII that was not extractable earlier becomes extractable later, because data seen downstream acts as a key to earlier sequences. Fine-tuning forges connections that did not exist before, exposing memorized content that no prior probing could surface. In Alignment Whack-a-Mole [preprint 2026], we push this onto commercial models: fine-tuning them to expand plot summaries into prose, an ordinary writing-assistant task, bypasses safety alignment and drives GPT-4o, Gemini-2.5-Pro, and DeepSeek-V3.1 to reproduce held-out copyrighted books from parametric memory, with recall generalizing across authors the model was never tuned on. Taken together, these results reframe emergent misalignment not as a sudden leap in capability but as a memorization phenomenon: fine-tuning surfaces latent structure the model already held. Memorization is a dynamic property of the whole training pipeline, and emergent misalignment is one of its downstream impacts."
